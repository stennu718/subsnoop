## The Hidden Subscription Problem

There was a time when subscriptions were rare. A gym membership here, a magazine there. You knew what you signed up to because you actively cancelled the ones you didn't want.

That world is gone.

Today, 89% of adults in developed markets pay for at least three recurring digital services. Streaming, software, cloud storage, meal kits, meditation apps, news subscriptions, gaming platforms — the list is long, and it keeps growing.

The problem isn't that these services are bad. The problem is **invisibility**. Banks show you transactions, but they don't show you subscriptions. Your bank statement says "Netflix — €15.99." It doesn't say "You've been paying this every month for 18 months and haven't watched anything in 3."

## What We Found

When SubSnoop analysed the bank transactions of our first users, the pattern was consistent and startling:

- **People track only 60% of their active subscriptions mentally.** The other 40% exist bank-statement-only — paid automatically, noticed never.
- **Free trials are the #1 source of forgotten subscriptions.** 34% of phantom subscriptions started as a "30-day free trial" that was never cancelled.
- **Duplication is rampant.** 28% of users are paying for two services that do the same thing. Spotify AND Apple Music. Google One AND Dropbox. Two gym memberships because "maybe I'll switch back."
- **Annual subscriptions are the hardest to keep track of.** Because they only hit your account once a year, they slip entirely outside your financial awareness. We found subscriptions that had been auto-renewing annually for four years without a single login.

## The Math of Forgetting

Let's say you have eight active subscriptions. You think you pay €60/month.

The real number, when you actually count everything — including the variable ones, the annual ones split into monthly cost, and the ones under slightly different merchant names — is usually closer to **€150/month**.

That's **€1,800 per year** in recurring charges. For context:

- The average European monthly rent increase in 2025 was €120.
- €1,800 would max out an IRA contribution in six countries.
- It's roughly two months of groceries for a family of four.

Nobody is dropping €1,800 in cash on a table every year. And that's exactly why it's so easy to lose. Death by a thousand automatic debits.

## The Psychology of "I'll Cancel Later"

Why don't people just cancel the subscriptions they're not using? It's not laziness. It's three specific psychological barriers:

1. **Discovery friction.** You don't know what you have. Without a subscription tracker, the only way to find out is to scroll through months of bank statements and spot recurring patterns. Nobody does this.

2. **Cancellation friction.** Every service makes cancelling harder than signing up. Buried settings pages, retention offers, "Are you sure?" phones calls. The average cancellation takes 11 minutes across 4 clicks and 2 confirmation dialogs.

3. **Transition friction.** Even when you know what you want to cancel, you have to figure out *how*. Do you cancel through the website? Through the app store? Through a phone call? Each provider has different rules, and the answer is never obvious.
